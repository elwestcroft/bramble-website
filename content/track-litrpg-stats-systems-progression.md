# How to Keep Track of LitRPG Stats, Systems, and Progression

> **TL;DR:** Track six ledgers, each with change history: stats, skills, levels and XP, inventory, currencies, and the System's own rules. Snapshot every character sheet at the end of each book, and never do level math in your head. Your readers have spreadsheets.

You need to track six things with database-grade reliability: character stats, skills and abilities, levels and experience, inventory, currencies, and the System's own rules. LitRPG readers are the most continuity-sensitive audience in fiction, because the genre's promise is a consistent system, and they arrive with spreadsheets, like auditors, which is essentially what they are. They will notice when your protagonist's Strength drops ten points between chapters. They will also notice the cooldown.

This guide covers what to track, how to structure it, and how to keep a progression fantasy honest across hundreds of chapters and one increasingly suspicious comment section.

## Why LitRPG Tracking Is Harder Than Normal Character Tracking

A regular novelist tracks facts that change occasionally. You track values that change constantly, by design, because change is the product. Every fight can touch HP, XP, and durability. Every level-up touches multiple stats. Every loot drop touches inventory. Multiply by a supporting cast with their own sheets, then by a Royal Road schedule of several chapters a week, and memory-based tracking fails inside a single arc. The genre quietly requires you to become your own game database administrator, a job you did not apply for.

## The Six Ledgers Every LitRPG Author Needs

1. **Stat sheets.** Current values for every statted character, with a change history. History is the part most authors skip and most need. When a reader asks why Strength was 34 in chapter 80 and 31 in chapter 95, you need to know whether that is a debuff you wrote or an error you shipped, and you would prefer to know before they do.
2. **Skills and abilities.** Name, rank, effect, cost, cooldown, and acquisition chapter. Cooldowns and costs generate more reader-reported errors than anything else in the genre.
3. **Levels and XP.** The curve itself (how much XP each level requires) plus each character's position on it. If your math is visible, readers will check it. Some will build spreadsheets. Yours has to be better than theirs, which is a strange arms race to be in, but here we are.
4. **Inventory.** Plot-relevant items only, with acquisition and loss points. Nobody cares about the 400th rat pelt. Everyone cares about the artifact sword you forgot the protagonist sold.
5. **Currencies and resources.** Gold, mana crystals, contribution points, whatever your economy runs on. An untracked economy drifts, and a drifting economy quietly breaks your stakes.
6. **System rules.** The constitution of your world: how the System works, what it forbids, the edge cases you have already ruled on. Every rule you print is a promise. Keep the promises in one list and reread it before writing exploits.

## Stat Blocks in the Manuscript Are Data, Not Decoration

The genre's signature blue boxes are also a tracking opportunity. If your stat blocks are formatted text pasted into the manuscript, every one of them is a fresh chance to typo a number that contradicts your ledger. In a genre where the interface is the aesthetic, that link is the difference between decoration and a source of truth.

## Progression Across a Long Series

Progression fantasy runs long: five, ten, fifteen books. Three habits keep a long system coherent. Snapshot every character sheet at the end of each book, so each new book starts from a known state. Log rule additions with their chapter, because the System you have at Book 8 is an accumulation of rulings and you will need the case law. And track your power ceiling on purpose. Write down where the top of your world sits, so escalation stays a choice instead of an accident you explain in an author's note.

## FAQ

**What is the best software for writing LitRPG?**
Generic writing tools handle prose and ignore systems, which is half the genre. Look for structured character tracking, in-manuscript stat blocks, and change history. Bramble was built with LitRPG authors as a primary audience, stat boxes and all.

**How do I keep XP math consistent?**
Define the curve once, write it in your system rules ledger, and never do level math in your head mid-chapter. Check every visible number against the curve before publishing.

**Do readers really audit stats?**
Yes. Royal Road comment sections routinely include reconstructed spreadsheets of protagonist stats. Treat your most obsessive reader as your QA department and stay one step ahead of them.

**Should I track side characters' stats too?**
Track any character whose numbers appear in print. An untracked printed number is a future error with a publication date.
