# What Is a Series Bible? (And Why You Need One by Book Two)

> **TL;DR:** A series bible is the master record of everything established as true in your story world (characters, places, rules, timeline, storylines), tagged to where each fact appears in print. Start it during Book 1, capture facts the moment you invent them, and never overwrite an old truth: date the change instead.

A series bible is a master reference recording everything established as true in your story world: characters and their details, locations, world rules, the timeline, and the status of every storyline, tagged to where each fact appears in print. Television writing rooms invented the form so a rotating staff could keep a show consistent. Novelists adopted it because a multi-book series has the same problem with a staff of one, and the one keeps forgetting things between seasons.

## What Goes in a Series Bible

The working sections, in rough order of how often you'll open them:

1. **Characters.** Every named character: appearance facts stated in print, relationships, motivation, and a status history (alive, located, titled, injured) with book and chapter references.
2. **Locations.** Named places with their printed details and the travel times between them.
3. **World rules.** Magic systems, technologies, laws, economies, and above all their constraints. This section is a list of promises you have made to strangers, in writing.
4. **Timeline.** When events happen relative to each other, backstory included. Timeline drift is the subtlest continuity error and the hardest to fix after print.
5. **Storylines.** Every open thread, its current state, and what each book did to it. This section is what stands between you and the dropped subplot.
6. **Canon ledger.** The unglamorous core: facts as printed, not as intended. When your notes and your print disagree, print wins, so the bible records print.

## When to Start One

During Book 1, the moment you suspect a sequel. The economics are lopsided in a way that should alarm you. Capturing a fact while drafting costs seconds. Reconstructing a bible from published books costs weeks of rereading your own work with a highlighter, and a few famous authors have ended up consulting fan wikis to find out what they themselves invented, which must be a strange afternoon. The bible you build as you go costs almost nothing. The one you build later costs a book's worth of writing time.

## The Two Habits That Make a Bible Work

**Capture at creation.** When the draft invents a fact, record it in the same session. Deferred record-keeping is abandoned record-keeping, and it defers very smoothly.

**Version the truth.** Do not overwrite facts when they change. Date the changes. A duke who dies in Book 3 should show as alive through Book 2 and dead after chapter 31, because "what did readers know at that point" is the question you will ask more than any other.

## FAQ

**Do I need a series bible for a standalone novel?**
A lighter version, often called a story bible, pays off for any novel with a large cast or a built world. If a sequel is even possible, build it properly from the start.

**Is a series bible the same as an outline?**
No. An outline plans what will happen; a bible records what is established. Outlines are disposable, bibles are permanent, and a mature series leans far harder on the bible.

**How long should a series bible be?**
As short as completeness allows. A bible is a retrieval tool, not a creative work, and every entry no scene touches is weight without value.
