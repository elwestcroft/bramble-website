# How to Plan a Book Series: Bibles, Arcs, and Continuity

> **TL;DR:** Decide five things before Book 1 ships: the series promise, the scope, the escalation ceiling, the load-bearing mysteries, and the ending's shape. Give every book its own complete arc plus one permanent move of the series arc, and build the bible while you draft, not after.

Planning a series comes down to three jobs. You decide the series-level promises before you finish Book 1, you structure each book as a complete story that also moves the larger arc, and you build a bible that grows with every draft. You do not need five books outlined in detail, and the writers who claim to have done this are usually lying to one of us. You do need to know what you're promising readers and which facts you've committed to, because a series is a long contract and your readers keep a copy.

## Decide These Five Things Before Book 1 Ships

Once Book 1 is in print, its facts are permanent. Before that happens, choose deliberately:

1. **The series promise.** The question the whole series answers, or the transformation it delivers. Every book advances it, and the last book pays it off.
2. **The scope.** Trilogy, open-ended saga, or episodic series in a shared world. A trilogy front-loads the payoff planning. An open series front-loads a world and cast that can generate conflict for as long as you need them to, which may be a decade.
3. **The power and stakes ceiling.** Decide where this world can escalate to before you spend any of it. Series die when Book 2 blows the escalation Book 6 was counting on.
4. **The load-bearing mysteries.** Whatever you dangle in Book 1, you owe readers an answer. Know the answers before you print the questions, or at minimum know which questions are holding up the roof.
5. **The ending's shape.** Not the details, just the shape. A writer who knows the final image writes middles that point somewhere, and it shows.

## Structure: Every Book Complete, Every Book a Step

The craft standard is the dual arc: each book resolves its own story question while advancing the series question. Readers will forgive an unresolved series arc. A book with no ending they will remember for years, and mention. In practice this means every book gets its own beginning, middle, and climax, moves the series arc once in a way that sticks, and closes on a hook that reopens tension without cheating the ending you just delivered.

## Continuity: The Series Bible Is Not Optional

By Book 2 you're writing two things at once: new story, and compliance with old story. The second job needs a series bible, one home for characters, locations, world rules, timeline, and storylines, recording what you stated in print and when you stated it. Build it during Book 1, while everything is fresh. Reconstructing a bible from published books means weeks of rereading your own work with a highlighter, and a few famous authors have ended up consulting fan wikis to find out what they themselves invented, which is its own kind of ghost story.

Two habits matter more than any structural choice. Record facts in the same session that invents them. And keep the history when facts change, because a bible that shows only the current state cannot answer "what did readers know at the end of Book 2," and that is half of everything you will ever ask it. ## Track Storylines, Not Just Books

A series carries threads that span volumes: the slow-burn romance, the background conspiracy, the mentor's secret. A thread that lives only in your head will get dropped, and dropped threads are the most-cited complaint in long-series reviews, right ahead of the eye colors. List every open thread, note what each book did to it, and reread the list when you outline the next book. ## Plan for the Reread Gap

Your readers wait a year between your books and remember less than you think. So do you, which is somehow more embarrassing. Give yourself a refresher before drafting (Bramble opens each book with a Previously On recap), and re-establish key facts for readers in early chapters through action rather than summary.

## FAQ

**Should I outline the entire series before writing Book 1?**
Outline the promise, the ceiling, and the ending's shape. Detailed multi-book outlines rarely survive contact with drafting. The five decisions above are the durable core.

**How is planning a series different from planning a standalone?**
A standalone spends everything it has. A series budgets: escalation, revelations, and character growth get rationed across books, and every printed fact becomes a constraint you live with, like a roommate.

**When should I start my series bible?**
During Book 1. Capturing facts as you draft costs minutes per session. Reconstruction costs weeks.
