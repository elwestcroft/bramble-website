# Writing Sprints: How Timed Sessions Beat Waiting for Motivation

> **TL;DR:** A sprint is 15 to 30 timed minutes with one rule: words go forward and editing waits. Set a target before you start, forbid the backspace, and record the number at the bell. Connect sprints to goals and history, and the trick becomes a practice.

A writing sprint is a short, timed writing session (commonly 15 to 30 minutes) with one rule: words go forward and editing waits. Sprints work because they flip the usual order of operations. You do not wait for motivation to start writing. You start writing, and motivation shows up around minute eight, looking a little sheepish about the delay. The timer manufactures the momentum you were planning to wait for.

## Why Sprints Work

Three mechanisms, all of them boring and all of them effective. **Bounded commitment:** "write the novel" is unstartable, "write for twenty minutes" is startable, and starting is most of the battle. **Deferred judgment:** the no-editing rule silences your inner critic by appointment. You are not writing badly; you are sprinting, and cleanup has its own slot on the calendar. **Fast feedback:** a sprint ends with a number, and a number that arrives within the hour beats a payoff that arrives at publication, as every abandoned manuscript in every drawer quietly attests.

The same mechanisms explain why sprints appear in every productivity tradition under different names, and why ADHD writing communities swear by them: bounded, visible, low-stakes units of work are the format the brain says yes to. (More in our writing with ADHD guide.)

## How to Run a Sprint

1. **Pick the unit.** Twenty to 25 minutes is the popular default. Drafting-heavy writers often prefer 15, deep-focus writers 45. Experiment for a week and keep whichever one you actually finish.
2. **Set the target before starting.** A scene, a beat, or a word count. A vague sprint leaks. A targeted sprint lands.
3. **Start the timer and forbid the backspace.** Typos stay. Bracketed placeholders ([HER NAME], [FIX THIS]) absorb every research urge, because research mid-sprint is procrastination with a library card.
4. **Stop at the bell and record the number.** The record is the point. A visible history of sessions is what turns sprints from a trick into a practice.
5. **Rest for real, then repeat if you have another in you.** Two honest sprints beat four depleted ones.

## Common Sprint Failures and Fixes

**"I edit anyway."** Shrink the sprint until the no-editing rule is survivable. Ten disciplined minutes beat thirty contested ones. **"My numbers make me feel bad."** Compare sessions to your own baseline, never to strangers on the internet, whose numbers are self-reported anyway. A sprint's only competitor is the blank page it beat. **"I sprint, then vanish for two weeks."** Normal, and survivable. Build for cheap returns (recaps, visible goals waiting patiently) rather than perfect streaks. The practice that forgives gaps is the practice that outlives them.

## FAQ

**How long should a writing sprint be?**
Fifteen to 30 minutes suits most writers. The right length is the longest one you can finish without editing.

**How many words should a sprint produce?**
Drafting speeds land anywhere from 200 to 800 words per 20 minutes, and the range is enormous by genre and writer. Track your own average and improve against it.

**Are group sprints better than solo?**
Group sprints add accountability and company. Solo sprints add scheduling freedom. Many writers mix both, and the timer is the active ingredient either way.
